1 - Forces in Nature

By the end of this topic, students should be able to:

  • Define the term force
  • Distinguish between fundamental forces and non-fundamental forces
  • Identify and describe the four fundamental forces of nature, stating their relative strengths, ranges, and mediating particles
  • Apply relevant physical laws and equations to analyze interactions involving gravitational, electric, and magnetic forces
  • Use correct SI units, symbols, and physical constants in calculations and explanations

2 - Electrostatics

Upon completing this topic, you should be able to:

  • Define the concept of electrostatics
  • Explain the law of conservation of electric charge in closed systems
  • Classify various materials as conductors, insulators, or semiconductors based on the mobility of their internal charge carriers
  • Describe the microscopic process of electron transfer that occurs during charging by friction (the triboelectric effect)
  • Differentiate between charging by conduction and charging by induction
  • Illustrate the role of "grounding" (earthing) in the process of charging a conductor by induction
  • Predict the behavior and distribution of charges when a charged object is brought near a neutral conductor versus a neutral insulator

3 - Coulomb's Law and Principle of Superposition

Upon successful completion of this topic, you should be able to:

  • Define Coulomb's inverse-square law
  • Explain the relationship between electric force, charge magnitude, and separation distance
  • Distinguish between attractive and repulsive electric interactions based on the signs of electric charges
  • Apply Coulomb's law to calculate the electric force between two point charges
  • Represent electric forces as vectors, indicating correct magnitude and direction
  • Use vector addition methods to determine the resultant electric force due to multiple charges
  • Explain the principle of superposition as applied to electric forces
  • Calculate the net electric force acting on a charge due to two or more surrounding charges
  • Solve numerical problems involving multiple charge configurations in one-dimensional and two-dimensional arrangements
  • Predict qualitative behavior of electric forces in simple electrostatic systems

4 - Electric Field and Electric Potential

By the end of this topic, students should be able to:

  • Define electric field and electric potential and explain their physical meanings in electrostatics
  • Describe the electric field produced by point charges and illustrate electric field lines for simple charge configurations
  • Calculate the electric field intensity due to a single point charge and multiple point charges using the principle of superposition
  • Represent electric fields as vectors, indicating correct magnitude and direction at a given point in space
  • Explain the concept of electric potential energy and its relationship to electric potential
  • Calculate electric potential due to point charges and systems of charges
  • Relate electric field and electric potential using mathematical expressions and interpret their physical significance
  • Solve numerical problems involving electric field, electric potential, and potential energy in one-dimensional and two-dimensional configurations

5 - Gauss's Law

Upon completing this topic, you should be able to:

  • Define the concept of electric flux
  • State Gauss's law in its integral form, identifying the relationship between net electric flux and the total enclosed charge
  • Explain the criteria for selecting an appropriate Gaussian surface based on the symmetry of a given charge distribution
  • Apply Gauss's law to calculate the electric field intensity
  • Calculate the electric field produced by an infinitely long line of charge or a charged cylinder using cylindrical symmetry
  • Derive the expression for the electric field near an infinite non-conducting sheet of charge using planar symmetry
  • Analyze the behavior of excess charges and electric fields within and on the surface of conductors in electrostatic equilibrium
  • Evaluate real-world applications of Gauss's law, such as the principles behind electrostatic shielding and the Faraday cage
